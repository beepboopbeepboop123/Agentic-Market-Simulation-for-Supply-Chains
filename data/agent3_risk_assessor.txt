### **Risk Assessment for Top 3 Routes (Shanghai Port Closure)**

#### **1. Route 12: Shanghai → Busan → New York**
- **Congestion Risk:** **High** – Busan Port is a major hub; rerouted Shanghai cargo may overwhelm capacity.
- **Geopolitical Risk:** **Medium** – U.S.-China tensions could delay customs in New York.
- **Seasonal Risk:** **Low** – No major seasonal disruptions expected.
- **Capacity Risk:** **High** – Busan’s infrastructure may struggle with sudden volume spikes.
- **Overall Risk:** **High**

#### **2. Route 16: Shanghai → Busan → Seattle → Chicago → New York**
- **Congestion Risk:** **Medium** – Seattle is less congested than Busan but may face inland rail delays.
- **Geopolitical Risk:** **Low** – U.S.-Canada trade relations are stable.
- **Seasonal Risk:** **Medium** – Winter weather could slow rail transit from Seattle to Chicago.
- **Capacity Risk:** **Medium** – Seattle’s port can handle increased traffic but may face inland bottlenecks.
- **Overall Risk:** **Medium**

#### **3. Route 3: Shanghai → Busan → Seattle → Chicago → New York**
- **Congestion Risk:** **Medium** – Similar to Route 16 but with an extra warehouse stop, adding minor risk.
- **Geopolitical Risk:** **Low** – Same as Route 16.
- **Seasonal Risk:** **Medium** – Winter rail delays remain a concern.
- **Capacity Risk:** **Medium** – Extra handling at Chicago warehouse may slow transit.
- **Overall Risk:** **Medium**

### **Safest Route Overall: Route 16**
**Why?** While all routes face risks, Route 16 balances congestion, geopolitical, and seasonal factors best. Seattle’s port is more resilient than Busan’s under sudden demand, and inland rail transit is less weather-sensitive than New York’s port operations. The route avoids the highest-risk congestion points while maintaining efficiency.
